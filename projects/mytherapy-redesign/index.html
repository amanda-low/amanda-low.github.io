01 · Problem

Hypertension is a silent condition.

Most people with high blood pressure don't feel sick. The medication doesn't make them feel better, side effects can make them feel worse, and unlike painkillers the meds don't pay off in real time. Motivation has to come from somewhere else.

MyTherapy is built like a task tracker. Hypertension management is a condition-control loop: take the pill, measure the outcome, see the trend, adjust with a GP. The app gets the first dose. It doesn't earn the next 90 days.

02 · Research

How I evaluated it.

A persona walkthrough as Margaret across all 21 onboarding screens, a four-lens expert review, and a comparative analysis against three benchmark apps.

The user I designed for

Margaret, 58. Newly diagnosed stage-1 hypertension, prescribed Lisinopril 10 mg daily. First-time medication tracker. Anxious about the diagnosis, wears reading glasses, sceptical of gamification.

A persona walkthrough, not a moderated session with real users. I played Margaret across the live MyTherapy iOS flow, screen by screen, scoring trust, comprehension, motivation, perceived medical credibility, and fit for actual needs. A stand-in for the moderated round I'd run with a real team.

5/10 overall. Strong on visuals, weak on content.

The daily reminder loop

The reminder, the action sheet, and what the user sees after they've taken their pill. Three screens, one moment.

MyTherapy

  • After-tap landing. Streak celebration, "1 completed task," a daily picture to reveal. Gamified, off-tone for a hypertension user.

Apple Health

03 · Principles

Five principles.

A medical reminder for a chronic condition has three jobs: get the dose taken today, build the daily habit, and keep the reason for taking the tablet alive in the user's head. Three of these principles answer those jobs. Two are product calls that make the rest possible.

Three behaviour-change bets

Established principles picked for where MyTherapy underperforms.

01

Show clear goals and progress.

A concrete BP target threaded through every surface, so the user always has an answer to "is this working?"

02

Write humane, motivating copy.

Recoverable language. A hollow dot says "you missed Tuesday." A streak loss says "you failed."

03

Design around cues, not willpower.

Reminders that thread the user's goal, not bare alarm clocks. "Day 14. Down 6 points, closer to your target."

Two product calls

Structural decisions that let the three above land.

04

Personalise by indication.

Once the user names their condition, the next step should be named too. Hypertension means BP tracking is the obvious move, not one tile of four.

05

Keep clinical surfaces clean.

Commerce is real, but it doesn't belong on Today, Progress, or Support. Move it to its own tab so users know which surface is selling and which is helping.

Style

Every colour shifts one half-step warmer than the clinical default. Trustworthy, not a waiting room.

  • Coral accent, not red.
  • Teal-green CTAs. Pharmacy, not wellness app.
  • Peach background. Low-stimulation.
  • Cream cards, not white. Softer for older eyes.
  • Warm brown ink, not black. Passes AA.

Illustration

Hand-drawn, editorial, calm. One tonal family across every scene.

  • No hexagonal containers. They read as ID badges.
  • No sparkles or bright yellow. Too stimulating.
  • People Margaret can see herself in. Calm, not abstract.

07 · AI

Targeted AI, not a chatbot.

In adherence apps "AI" can mean anything from real ML to rule-based reminders.

Predictive lapse detection

  • Medisafe's JITI: ML trained on 12+ years of behavioural data predicts who's about to skip a dose.
  • In MyTherapy: a push only when Margaret is genuinely off-pattern. Less noise, more signal.

Pattern detection on Insights

  • Bearable: correlates adherence with symptoms, sleep, and mood.
  • In MyTherapy: one plain-language observation a week. "Your evening readings run about 6 points higher than mornings. That's normal. Tell your GP if it changes."

Drug-aware microcopy

  • Where the field is thin: most apps flag a missed dose, none tell you what to do about it.
  • In MyTherapy: for Lisinopril, "Don't double up today. Take today's dose at 08:30 as usual." For the 10 to 15% who get a dry cough, name the chance and route to the GP.

08 · Validation and metrics

Testing and metrics.

What I'd take into the first week with a real team.

Hypotheses

Three bets the redesign rests on. Each one has a target delta and a test that would prove or kill it.

H1

Naming BP tracking as the next step lifts day-30 retention.

  • Target: a meaningful increase in day-30 retention for the hypertension cohort. Single-screen change on the highest-leverage moment in onboarding, so the delta should be the largest of the three.
  • Test: A/B on the post-medication screen. Control, the four-tile hub. Variant, confirm + named next step (log BP).

H2

A weekly adherence score out-performs the streak on the bad week.

  • Why it matters: the streak's failure mode is the metric that counts. What happens after a miss.
  • Test: A/B on the Today home, segmented to users with at least one missed dose in week one. Primary metric, same-week return rate.

H3

Threading the user's lifestyle goal through the daily loop lifts retention beyond just letting them set one.

  • Target: a measurable increase in day-30 retention. Smaller than H1, since the effect compounds over weeks.
  • Test: A/B where both groups pick a goal. Control sees generic reminder and milestone copy. Variant sees the goal threaded back into both. Isolates the surfacing from the picking.

How I'd validate

Qualitative

Two rounds with real users

  • Moderated usability, 6 to 8 newly diagnosed hypertension patients aged 50+. Can they reach a logged BP reading without help, and do they understand why BP tracking is the recommended next step?
  • Diary study, 4 weeks, 5 patients on the live build. Do day-30 retention and BP logging frequency hold up outside a moderated room?

Quantitative

Three metrics, in order of how soon they show up

  • First BP reading logged in onboarding. The earliest signal that the reframe lands.
  • Day-30 retention for hypertension users. The headline metric.
  • GP export rate. The clinical-value indicator. Small absolute volume, but the one a clinician would care about.

If I had two sprints

Sprint 1

  • Replace the streak with the weekly adherence score.
  • Ship the screen after the first medication.
  • Surface BP logging on Today.
  • A/B test H1 and H2.

Sprint 2

  • Insights trend chart.
  • Missed-dose recovery copy. Smart static logic, no AI yet.
  • Move commerce off Support into its own tab.

v2

  • Pattern detection on Insights.
  • Drug-aware recovery copy.
  • Pre-appointment summaries.

Sprints 1 and 2 are roughly 60% of the retention upside without any AI infrastructure.

What I'd do differently

A home assignment is a structured argument from outside the team. Three things I'd change the moment I had real users, a clinician, and a backlog.

  • Test the weekly adherence score earlier than anything else. It's the bet of the redesign, and the persona walkthrough can't tell me whether it lands. The first user who misses two doses is the one who decides.
  • Pressure-test the goal-threading copy with a clinician. "Travel without worry" is a felt outcome. A GP would tell me which framings are safe, which over-promise, and which cross a regulatory line I can't see from the outside.
  • Treat the persona walkthrough as scaffolding, not evidence. Margaret is a useful stand-in for shaping the argument. She's not a substitute for six newly diagnosed patients in a room. Some of what reads as a clear win on paper will look different on day three.
