01 · Problem

Hypertension is a silent condition.

Most people with high blood pressure don't feel sick. The medication doesn't make them feel better, side effects can make them feel worse, and unlike painkillers the meds don't pay off in real time. Motivation has to come from somewhere else.

Where MyTherapy falls short

The outcome is invisible. The app counts pills. It doesn't count the BP numbers a patient and their GP actually care about.

The GP loop is missing. No goal, no follow-up date, no shareable summary. The reason most people install the app in the first place.

The streak is brittle. One missed dose breaks it. Literature on chronic-disease adherence is clear: streak loss produces shame, not better behaviour.

Trust leaks. RedPoints, sponsored content, pharmacy commerce on the Support tab. Each is defensible alone. Stacked, they undermine the medical credibility this user came for.

02 · Research

How I evaluated it.

A persona walkthrough as Margaret across all 21 onboarding screens, a four-lens expert review, and a comparative analysis against three benchmark apps.

The user I designed for

Margaret, 58. Newly diagnosed stage-1 hypertension, prescribed Lisinopril 10 mg daily. First-time medication tracker. Anxious about the diagnosis, wears reading glasses, sceptical of gamification.

Method 1

Emulated usability test

A persona walkthrough, not a moderated session with real users. I played Margaret across the live MyTherapy iOS flow, screen by screen, scoring trust, comprehension, motivation, perceived medical credibility, and fit for actual needs. A stand-in for the moderated round I'd run with a real team.

5/10 overall. Strong on visuals, weak on content.

Method 2

Behaviour-change review

A behaviour-change lens applied across the live MyTherapy iOS flow, scoring motivation, cues, and shame against the literature on chronic-disease adherence.

4/10 on behaviour-change. Streaks and points are the main drag.

Three findings

The screen after the first medication is the highest-leverage in the app.

Margaret picks Hypertension as the indication. The app drops her on a generic "1 of 4 categories" hub. The obvious next move (track BP) is buried as one of four equal tiles. This single screen change drives most of the redesign.

Streaks and points are clinically risky for chronic conditions.

An all-or-nothing streak silently incentivises double-dosing or delayed dosing. Both are explicit don'ts for Lisinopril.

The outcome the user cares about is invisible.

Margaret installed the app to manage her BP. The app counts pills. There's no BP card on Today, no trend chart on Progress, and no way to share with her GP. Day 30 has nothing to come back for.

03 · Principles

Five principles.

A medical reminder for a chronic condition has three jobs: get the dose taken today, build the daily habit, and keep the reason for taking the tablet alive in the user's head. Three of these principles answer those jobs. Two are product calls that make the rest possible.

Three behaviour-change bets

Three established design-for-behaviour-change principles, picked because they map directly to areas where the original MyTherapy underperforms.

01

Show clear goals and progress.

  • Concrete BP target (under 130 systolic), threaded through every surface.
  • Weekly dot strip, four-week trend chart, and Day-14 milestone screen all show movement against the goal.

Where MyTherapy falls short: counts pills, not BP. No target, no trend, no answer to "is this working?" Sources: Michie et al., The Behaviour Change Wheel (COM-B); Locke and Latham on goal salience.

02

Write humane, motivating copy.

  • Avoid shaming. Normalise lapses.
  • Recoverable language: a hollow dot says "you missed Tuesday." A streak loss says "you failed."

Where MyTherapy falls short: streak counters and loss-framed prompts punish the bad week, when the user most needs to come back. Sources: Wohl et al. on the "what-the-hell effect"; Haynes et al., Cochrane review of adherence interventions.

03

Design around cues, not willpower.

  • Contextual reminders that thread the user's goal, not bare alarm clocks.
  • Day one: "The one that brings your numbers down."
  • Steady state: "Day 14. Down 6 points, closer to your target."

Where MyTherapy falls short: alarm-clock copy with no goal context. The prompt fades into background noise by week three. Sources: WHO, Adherence to Long-Term Therapies (2003); NICE NG76, Medicines adherence.

Two product calls

Not behaviour-change theory, but the structural decisions that let the three above land.

04

Personalise by indication.

Once the user names their condition, the next step should be named too. Hypertension means BP tracking is the obvious next move, not one tile of four. Personalisation by indication is what makes "make the outcome visible" possible: you can't centre a trend the user never started logging.

05

Keep clinical surfaces clean.

Commerce is real, but it doesn't belong on Today, Progress, or Support. Move it to its own clearly named tab so users know which surface is selling and which is helping. Trust is the substrate every other principle is built on; a cluttered clinical surface erodes it faster than any single feature can rebuild it.

Style

Every colour shifts one half-step warmer than the clinical default. The system stays trustworthy without feeling like a waiting room.

  • Coral accent, not red. Same warmth, none of the medical-alert reading.
  • Teal-green CTAs (#0F4C3A). Closer to a pharmacy or GP portal than a wellness app.
  • Peach background, inherited from MyTherapy. Low-stimulation surface for an anxious user.
  • Cream cards, not pure white. Softer for older eyes, no glare around text.
  • Warm brown ink, not black. Passes AA contrast, reads softer on a warm ground.

Illustration

Hand-drawn, editorial, calm. Carries the warm palette through to every scene so the system stays in one tonal family.

  • No hexagonal containers. They read as ID badges. Soft peach cards with cream halos instead.
  • No sparkles or bright yellow. Both raise stimulation, the wrong cue for an anxious newly-diagnosed user.
  • People Margaret can see herself in. Silver hair, glasses, a coral top, holding her medication. Calm, not abstract.

07 · AI

Targeted AI, not a chatbot.

In adherence apps "AI" can mean anything from real ML to rule-based reminders. Medisafe and Bearable are the two with genuine models. MyTherapy today has neither. Four places I'd ship real AI.

Predictive lapse detection

  • Medisafe's JITI: ML trained on 12+ years of behavioural data predicts who's about to skip a dose.
  • In MyTherapy: a push only when Margaret is genuinely off-pattern. Less noise, more signal.

Pattern detection on Insights

  • Bearable: correlates adherence with symptoms, sleep, and mood.
  • In MyTherapy: one plain-language observation a week. "Your evening readings run about 6 points higher than mornings. That's normal. Tell your GP if it changes."

Drug-aware microcopy

  • Where the field is thin: most apps flag a missed dose, none tell you what to do about it.
  • In MyTherapy: for Lisinopril, "Don't double up today. Take today's dose at 08:30 as usual." For the 10 to 15% who get a dry cough, name the chance and route to the GP.

Pre-appointment summary

  • my a:care: reflects patterns to clinicians, but doesn't auto-prepare them.
  • In MyTherapy: three days before her GP visit, a one-page PDF. Weekly BP averages, readings outside target, adherence, side-effect notes, questions to ask.

What I would not use AI for: streaks, motivational pep talk, anthropomorphised "chat with your meds" (Finch's virtual-pet model works for self-care, not for chronic medication in an anxious 58-year-old), or drug interaction checks (those should be deterministic from a maintained database, not an LLM).

08 · Validation and metrics

Testing and metrics.

What I'd take into the first week with a real team.

Hypotheses

Two bets the redesign rests on. Each one has a target delta and a test that would prove or kill it.

H1

Naming BP tracking as the next step lifts day-30 retention.

  • Target: 10 to 15 point lift on day-30 retention for the hypertension cohort.
  • Test: A/B on the post-medication screen. Control, the four-tile hub. Variant, confirm + named next step (log BP).

H2

A recoverable dot strip out-performs the streak on the bad week.

  • Why it matters: the streak's failure mode is the metric that counts. What happens after a miss.
  • Test: A/B on the Today home, segmented to users with at least one missed dose in week one. Primary metric, same-week return rate.

How I'd validate

Qualitative

Two rounds with real users

  • Moderated usability, 6 to 8 newly diagnosed hypertension patients aged 50+. Can they reach a logged BP reading without help, and do they understand why BP tracking is the recommended next step?
  • Diary study, 4 weeks, 5 patients on the live build. Do day-30 retention and BP logging frequency hold up outside a moderated room?

Quantitative

Leading and lagging metrics

  • Leading: first BP reading logged in onboarding. The earliest signal that the reframe lands.
  • Lagging: day-30 retention for hypertension users. The headline metric.
  • Lagging: GP export rate. The clinical-value indicator. Small absolute volume, but the one a clinician would care about.

If I had two sprints

Sprint 1

  • Replace the streak with the weekly dot strip.
  • Ship the screen after the first medication.
  • Surface BP logging on Today.
  • A/B test H1 and H2.

Sprint 2

  • Insights trend chart.
  • Missed-dose recovery copy. Smart static logic, no AI yet.
  • Move commerce off Support into its own tab.

v2

  • Pattern detection on Insights.
  • Drug-aware recovery copy.
  • Pre-appointment summaries.

Sprints 1 and 2 are roughly 60% of the retention upside without any AI infrastructure.

What I'd watch in the first month with a real team

A home assignment ends here. The actual work begins when the first 100 hypertension users land on the new flow. Three things I'd be checking week one.

  • Are users finding "Log BP"? If not, the named next step needs to be louder, or the screen above it is doing too much.
  • What does the bad week actually look like? The dot strip is the bet of the redesign. The first user who misses two doses tells you whether it works.
  • Is the GP export getting opened? The clinical-value loop is the long-term retention story. If month-two users aren't reaching for it, the milestone screen is the lever to pull.
